## Context

A ~4,000-word piece written for people in tech enduring the season's traditional
annual planning process. It was prompted by an interview question and draws on the
author's ~25 years of variously painful planning experiences. It deliberately does
**not** claim to offer the one true way to plan — instead it offers things to think
about, starting from the question of *why planning is so often awful and useless*.

## Main Thesis

There is no "Right Way to Plan." Planning is like software: with enough effort
anything can be made to work, so the goal isn't a perfect process but **solving for a
set of constraints and for what we know about how humans actually behave**.
Trade-offs are the heart of engineering and engineering leadership — engineers
choose not between right and wrong answers but between trade-offs, and planning is no
different.

## Key Arguments

### Pure bottom-up planning doesn't work
- Bottom-up planning starts at the leaves of the hierarchy (squads/teams), then
  aggregates and synthesizes plans level by level as they move up.
- Individual teams almost by definition **lack the information and perspective** to
  know what matters most for the whole organization, so they make **locally optimal**
  choices based on what they can see.
- Bottom-up processes also **fall victim to anchoring**: when you ask people to spend
  energy advocating for what they want and the resources they need, they become
  **emotionally invested** in that outcome — and it becomes the **floor** for their
  expectations, making later adjustment painful.

### Provide frameworks and constraints (combine top-down + bottom-up)
- Leadership should hold a **strong opinion about the shape and goals** of the effort,
  with the details filled in by the teams.
- The fix isn't pure top-down either — it's pairing **top-down direction** with
  **bottom-up input**.

### Planning is the wrong time to introduce anything new
- Don't bolt on new processes, frameworks, or surprises during planning; it adds
  thrash when people most need stability.

### The dangerous "middle zone"
- The real trouble is the middle zone where teams **pretend to plan but do it badly**.
- Processes that strive for **false precision backfire**, generating thrash and anxiety
  rather than clarity.

### Often the best plan is to just try it
- For a large class of ideas, the better move is to **try them directly** — quick and
  dirty tests to validate or disprove the idea — **without asking for resources or
  cross-team support**, and being ready to throw the work away.
- This is frequently a great alternative to producing a "real" plan at all.

## Takeaways

1. **Don't chase the perfect process.** Solve for your constraints and for how humans
   actually behave.
2. **Treat trade-offs as the work**, not a sign something's wrong.
3. **Beware anchoring** in bottom-up advocacy — early asks become hard expectation
   floors.
4. **Lead with frameworks and constraints**, then let teams fill in the details.
5. **Keep planning boring** — it's the wrong time to introduce anything new.
6. **Avoid false precision**; it manufactures thrash and anxiety.
7. **When you can, experiment instead of plan** — cheap tests beat elaborate plans for
   many ideas.
